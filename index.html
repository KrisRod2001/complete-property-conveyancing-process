Understanding Property Conveyancing

Conveyancing is the legal process of transferring property ownership from one party to another. It involves detailed checks, documentation, and coordination to ensure a legally sound transaction.

While the core steps are similar across Australia, Victoria and Queensland have unique requirements that experienced conveyancers navigate expertly.

The Conveyancing Process When Buying

Key stages for purchasers in Victoria and Queensland:

  1. Contract Review: Your conveyancer reviews the Contract of Sale and Section 32 (VIC) or equivalent disclosures (QLD) before you sign.
  2. Searches & Inquiries: Title searches, council certificates, planning checks, and utility inquiries to uncover any issues.
  3. Finance Approval: Coordination with your lender for unconditional finance.
  4. Stamp Duty: Calculation and payment of duty, including any concessions or exemptions.
  5. Pre-Settlement Inspection: Final walkthrough to ensure property condition matches the contract.
  6. Settlement: Electronic settlement via PEXA, transfer of funds, and registration of title in your name.

The Conveyancing Process When Selling

Key responsibilities for vendors:

  1. Prepare Contract: Your conveyancer prepares the Contract of Sale and Vendor Statement (VIC) with all required disclosures.
  2. Negotiate Terms: Adjustments for rates, taxes, and any special conditions.
  3. Respond to Requisitions: Answer buyer inquiries about title and property.
  4. Discharge Mortgage: Arrange payoff of existing loans at settlement.
  5. Settlement Day: Transfer keys and receive proceeds after funds clear.

Key Differences Between Victoria and Queensland

While processes are similar, notable variations include:

  • Victoria requires a detailed Section 32 Vendor Statement
  • Queensland uses REIQ contracts with different cooling-off periods
  • Electronic conveyancing mandates and timelines vary
  • Stamp duty rates and first-home buyer concessions differ

Working with a conveyancer licensed in both states eliminates confusion.
